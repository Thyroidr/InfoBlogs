Game Theory in Competitive Programming

Posted on 2018-09-09 Edited on 2019-04-17 In Number Theory and Mathematics

Description

Game theory investigates the interaction of rational beings in different scenarios. Its application is most notable in economics and other social sciences, although it is extended into computer science and logic.

The typical game theory problems in competitive programming often require the program the determine the guaranteed winner of the game given certain information. Game theory problems do not appear frequently in programming contests, and when they do, they are often the Ad Hoc problem. Nonetheless, I personally find Game Theory, especially the Sprague-Grundy theorem, to be interesting. This post first introduces the reader to relatively simple games to help build the basic intuition. It then explains the Sprague-Grundy theorem with examples. In the end, several exercise problems are given, with solution or hint.

The Stones Game

Let us start Game Theory with a sample question. Hopefully you will be able to figure this quesiton out independently.

Problem Description

Welcome to the land of Melodramia, my dear friend! This is a land of magic and myths, of heroes and heroines. And this is also home to two snapdragons, Rose and Scarlet, who are the best of friends.

As some of you might know, Rose and Scarlet play a lot of games. Today, their game involves stones. To be exact, there are $n$ stones, each in its own pile. Rose and Scarlet take turns to combine two piles of stone at their will. As there can be a lot of stones, they agreed that no pile of stone should ever contain strictly more than $m$ stones (a pile can contain exactly $m$ stones). The first player who is unable to combine two piles loses. Surely, each player must combine two piles into one in a turn, and that there needs to be at least two piles of stones for a combination. Rose always takes the first turn.

After playing a few games, Rose and Scarlet have discovered a pattern abouth this game. They are certain that given $n$ and $m$, one player can win regardless of what the other player does, but they are not sure who is the winner. Find it out for them.

Input

Rose and Scarlet plan to play $t$ games, so the first line will contain $t (1 \le t \le 100)$.
The next $t$ lines will each contain two integers, $n$ and $m$ ($1 \le n, m \le 10^9$).

Output

The output should contain $t$ lines, each line contains $1$ or $0$, where $0$ means Rose wins, and $1$ means Scarlet wins.

Solution

We have two observations for this problem:

  1. The number of piles reduce by $1$ each turn.
  2. The number of piles in the end game state is always $\lfloor \frac {n-1}{m} \rfloor + 1$.

With 1. and 2. we can know the number of turns until the end game state, which is $n - (\lfloor \frac {n-1} {m} \rfloor + 1)$. If this value is odd, then Rose wins, otherwise Scarlet wins.

The Nim Game

The stone game above is an example of a simple game theory problem in competitive programming. Most problems, however, are more complex and often based on the Sprague-Grundy theorem. One cannot explain the Sprague-Grundy theorem without using the Nim game.

Game Description

There exist $n$ piles of stone, where the $i$-th pile has $a_i$ stones. Two players (let them be Rose and Scarlet, again) take turns to remove any number of stones from a pile of stone at their will. The player who takes the last stone loses. Below we have two games of Nim.

Game 1

Step Pile A Pile B Pile C Moves
1 1 1 2 Rose takes 1 from A
2 0 1 2 Scarlet takes 1 from C
3 0 1 1 Rose takes 1 from B
4 0 0 1 Scarlet takes 1 from C and loses

At step 4, Scarlet can only make one move, which results in a lose for her. Let us name this position $*0$, the losing position.

At step 3, Rose can make two moves: take 1 from B, or take 1 from C. These two moves can be considered the same. Let us name Rose's position *1, we know *1 = {*0}, meaning *1 leads to *0 for the next player.

At step 2, Scarlet can make three moves: take 1 from B, take 1 from C, or take 2 from C. Removing 1 from C leaves Rose in position *1; removing 2 from C leaves Rose in position *0; at last, removing 1 from B leaves Rose with a pile of 2 stones, where her position is *1 = {*0}, since she is rational and would not take 2 to lose. Hence, Scarlet's position at step 2 is {*1, *0}.

At step 1, Rose can make three different moves: take 1 from A or B; take 1 from C; take 2 from C. Taking 1 from A or B leaves Scarlet in {*1, *0}. Taking 1 from C leaves Scarlet one stone in each pile, where she is forced to take only from 1 from 1 pile (resulting in *1 for Rose as shown in step 3). Taking 1 from C hence leaves Scarlet in {*1}. Last but not least, taking 2 from C leaves Scarlet in *1. Rose's position in step 1 is hence

Game 2

Step Pile A' Pile B' Pile C' Moves
1 1 1 1 Rose takes 1 from A'
2 0 1 1 Scarlet takes 1 from B'
3 0 0 1 Rose takes 1 from A' and loses

We will analyse this game, just like we did to game 1.

At step 3, Rose's position is obviously *0.

At step 2, Scarlet only has one possible move, so her position is *1 = {*0}.

At step 1, Rose, again, only has one possible move, so her position is {*1}.
