Flow Network

Posted on 2018-09-17 Edited on 2019-04-17 In Graph Theory

Maximum Flow

Scenario

Problem Link
Every time it rains on Farmer John's fields, a pond forms over Bessie's favorite clover patch. This means that the clover is covered by water for awhile and takes quite a long time to regrow. Thus, Farmer John has built a set of drainage ditches so that Bessie's clover patch is never covered in water. Instead, the water is drained to a nearby stream. Being an ace engineer, Farmer John has also installed regulators at the beginning of each ditch, so he can control at what rate water flows into that ditch.

Farmer John knows not only how many gallons of water each ditch can transport per minute but also the exact layout of the ditches, which feed out of the pond and into each other and stream in a potentially complex network.

Given all this information, determine the maximum rate at which water can be transported out of the pond and into the stream. For any given ditch, water flows in only one direction, but there might be a way that water can flow in a circle.

Introduction

The problem above describes a graph with edges (drainge ditches) and vertices (Bessie's pond, intersection of ditches and the pond), and we are seeking how much water can flow from one one vertex to another one. This is a classic maximum flow problem.

A flow network is a directed graph $G=(V,E)$ satisfying:

  • each edge has a capacity and flow
  • there are at least two vertices, including one source and one sink.

In other words, a flow network is $G = ((V, E), s, t, c, f)$ where

  • $s$ and $t$ are the source and sink of the graph, respectively
  • $c(u,v)$ and $f(u,v)$ are the capacity and flow of the edge $(u, v)$, respectively

(Image: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinic%27s_algorithm))

Above is an example of a flow network. The regular vertices are numbered from $1$ to $4$, $s$ represents the source and $t$ represents the sink. The edges possess two properties, the flow and the capacity. Consider a pipe, the volume of water flowing in the pipe per second cannot exceed the maximum capacity of the pipe per second. Hence, in a flow network the flow of an edge never exceeds the capacity of the edge. If we imagine sink as tap that can output an infinite volume of water per second, and the sink as a blackhole, then the maximum flow of a flow network is the volume of water that can travel from the source to the sink, satisfying that no flow of an edge exceeds the capacity of that edge. There exist multiple algorithms to compute the maximum flow of a graph. Here we will introduce the Dinic's algorithm for it has a better time complexity (despite being slightly longer to code).

Applications

Bipartite Matching

The significance of an algorithm to the maximum flow problem is that many seemingly unrelated problems can be modelled to a flow graph and solved with the maximum flow algorithms. One example problem that can be solved by the maximum flow is maximum cardinality bipartite matching, where we look for the maximum number of edges without shared vertices in a bipartite graph. All vertices are either in $X$ or $Y$ in a bipartite graph, and there is no edge connecting any two vertices in the same set. This can be solved by connecting each vertex in $X$ with an edge of capacity $1$, and each vertex in $Y$ with $t$ with an edge of capacity $1$.

Minimum Path Cover in Directed Acyclic Graph

For each vertex $v$ in the graph, we can change it to two vertices, $v_in$ and $v_out$, and place the two vertices in two differenct sets to form a bipartite graph. By Konig's Theorem, let the maximum cardinality matching for the bipartite graph be $m$ and $n$ be the number of vertices in the original graph, then the minimum vertex-disjoint path is $n-m$.

Sample Problem

Problem Link
English Translation:
Given a grid of $m$ rows and $n$ columns where each cell contains a non-negative number. You can take number out of the grid from arbitrary cells until no two remaining numbers share a common edge. What the maximum of numbers you can take out.

Input

Each test case consists of multiple inputs. Each input includes $m$, $n$ and $m \times n$ non negative numbers.

Output

The maximum sum for each input.

Sample IO

See problem link.

Solution

We can paint the grid like a chessboard, such that cells in black are in set $X$ and cells in white are in set $Y$. We can then add vertices $s$ and $t$. Connect each vertex in $X$ with $s$ with an edge whose capacity equals the value in the corresponding cell. Connect each vertex in $Y$ with $t$ in the same fashion. Then, connect every pair of adjacent cells with an edge of infinite capacity. The sum of all numbers in the grid, subtracted by the minimum sum of numbers left in the grid, is the answer. The minimum sum, however, is minimum cut of the graph (since if we remove them from the original graph, $s$ would be disconnected with $t$). The minimum cut of a graph is equal in value to the maximum flow. Hence,

$$ Max. Taken out = Sum - Min. Remaining = Sum - Min. Cut = Sum - Max. Flow $$
